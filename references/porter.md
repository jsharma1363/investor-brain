# Porter's Competitive Strategy — Reference

Distilled from Competitive Strategy (1980), Competitive Advantage (1985), and the 2008 HBR
update. Integrated with Mauboussin's moat taxonomy and Cialdini's influence frameworks.

---

## Five Forces Framework

Industry profitability is determined by five forces. A wonderful business in a structurally
terrible industry will usually underperform a mediocre business in a great industry.

### 1. Threat of New Entrants
**Barriers that matter:**
- Supply-side economies of scale (fixed costs ÷ volume)
- Demand-side scale / network effects (value increases with users)
- Switching costs (data migration, retraining, integration)
- Capital requirements (especially unrecoverable: R&D, advertising)
- Incumbency advantages (proprietary tech, learning curve, brand, locations)
- Distribution access (locked-up channels)
- Government policy (licensing, patents, regulation)

**Retaliation signal:** History of aggressive response + excess resources to fight = low
entry threat even with low structural barriers.

### 2. Supplier Power
Suppliers capture value when: concentrated, not dependent on the industry, offer
differentiated inputs with switching costs, can credibly integrate forward.

**Labor as supplier:** Scarce skills + organization = bargaining power that erodes margins.

### 3. Buyer Power
Buyers capture value when: concentrated, products undifferentiated, low switching costs,
can integrate backward, product is large share of buyer's costs.

**Key nuance:** If the product critically affects the buyer's quality or performance, price
sensitivity drops regardless of other factors.

### 4. Substitutes
A substitute serves the same function by different means. Most dangerous when:
improving price-performance faster than the industry, produced by high-profit industries
(attracts investment), low switching costs.

**Substitutes come from unexpected places.** Always ask: what else solves this need?

### 5. Rivalry
Destructive when: many equal-sized players, slow growth, high exit barriers, price-based
competition, undifferentiated products, high fixed costs + low marginal costs.

**Price competition is the worst form** — transfers value directly to buyers. Competition
on features/service/brand can maintain or increase industry profitability.

## Generic Strategies

Three paths to above-average returns. Pursuing all three = "stuck in the middle" = below-
average profitability.

| Strategy | Source of Advantage | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Cost leadership** | Lowest cost at acceptable quality | Quality falls too far behind; imitation of scale |
| **Differentiation** | Unique value worth a premium | Premium exceeds buyer willingness; imitation narrows gap |
| **Focus** | Serve a narrow segment better than broad competitors | Segment erodes; broad competitors sub-segment |

**Investing application:** Identify which generic strategy the company pursues. Then ask:
is the advantage structural or behavioral? Cost leaders with scale advantages (Mauboussin
supply-side scale) are more durable than cost leaders who just cut corners. Differentiators
with switching costs + network effects are more durable than those with brand alone.

## Value Chain

Where in the chain does the company create value? Where is it vulnerable?

**Primary:** Inbound logistics → Operations → Outbound logistics → Marketing/Sales → Service
**Support:** Infrastructure, HR, Technology development, Procurement

**For investors:** A company may dominate operations but be weak in distribution. A competitor
may attack exactly that weak link. Understanding the value chain reveals which advantages
are structural and which are temporary. Also reveals where capex and R&D are deployed —
essential for ROIIC assessment.

## Industry Evolution

| Stage | Characteristics | Investor Signal |
|---|---|---|
| **Emerging** | High uncertainty, educating buyers, no scale yet | Path dependency — early choices lock in. High risk, high optionality. |
| **Growth → Maturity** | Slowing growth, buyers more demanding, overcapacity, price wars | Transition is where most value destruction happens. Quality of management matters most here. |
| **Decline** | Permanent demand drop (substitution, demographics) | Last profitable survivor wins. Exit barriers determine pain. |
| **Fragmented** | No firm has significant share | Ask: is this structural or can it be consolidated? |

**Maps to Mauboussin's Dickinson life cycle:** Use cash flow patterns (operating/investing/
financing signs) to classify where a company sits, then apply Porter's evolution logic.

## Competitor Analysis

For each competitor, build a profile:

1. **Goals** — What do they want? (market share, profitability, prestige, survival)
2. **Strategy** — How are they competing today? (which generic strategy, which value chain emphasis)
3. **Assumptions** — What do they believe about themselves and the industry? (often wrong → blind spots)
4. **Capabilities** — What can they actually do? (strengths and weaknesses by function)

**The edge:** Most investors analyze what competitors DO. Porter says analyze what they
BELIEVE and WANT — that predicts what they WILL do. Pair with Cialdini: commitment/
consistency means companies double down on stated strategies even when wrong.

## Porter → Mauboussin Bridge

Porter is industry-level (why some industries are profitable). Mauboussin is firm-level
(why some companies within an industry earn excess returns). They connect:

| Porter Force | Mauboussin Moat Source | Defense Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| New entrants | Supply-side scale | Unit costs decline with volume; entrants can't match |
| New entrants | Demand-side scale (network effects) | Value increases with users; entrants face cold-start |
| Buyer power | Switching costs | Data, workflow, training lock buyers in |
| Rivalry + substitutes | Pricing power / intangibles | Brand, patents, licenses create unreplicable differentiation |
| New entrants | Efficient scale | Market supports only 1-3 profitable players |

**When the frameworks disagree:** A company with high ROIC in a Porter-unfavorable industry
is either temporarily lucky or has a moat strong enough to override industry structure.
The investor must determine which — and the default assumption should be mean reversion
unless the moat evidence is overwhelming.

## Porter + Cialdini Integration

Porter assumes rational actors. Cialdini shows influence dynamics sustain irrational
competitive behavior:

- **Social proof** sustains brand moats beyond functional differentiation (buyers choose
  what others choose, even without quality difference)
- **Commitment/consistency** makes companies double down on failing strategies (sunk cost
  as identity, not just economics) — this is what creates the blind spots in Porter's
  competitor analysis framework
- **Switching costs are partly psychological**, not just economic. Commitment/consistency
  bias makes the *perceived* cost of switching higher than the actual cost.
- **Authority** sustains pricing power beyond product quality (trusted brand as authority
  figure in the category)
- **Scarcity** drives panic buying during supply crunches (compute, chips) beyond rational
  inventory management — relevant to current AI infrastructure cycle

## Quick Reference: Five Forces Applied to Investing

When evaluating any business, run the five forces check:

| Force | Key Question | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| New entrants | What stops someone from replicating this business? | Low barriers + high returns = returns will compress |
| Suppliers | Does the company depend on a concentrated supplier? | Single-source risk, margin squeeze |
| Buyers | Can customers easily switch or bargain? | Undifferentiated product, large concentrated buyers |
| Substitutes | What else solves this need — even from a different industry? | Improving substitute on a better trajectory |
| Rivalry | Is competition price-based or value-based? | Price wars in commodity markets with high fixed costs |

**The five forces check is a COMPLEMENT to Mauboussin's moat analysis, not a replacement.**
Porter tells you whether the industry is attractive. Mauboussin tells you whether the
specific company can sustain excess returns within that industry. Both are required.
