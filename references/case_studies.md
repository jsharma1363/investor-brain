# Investment Case Studies

Real investments from Buffett's letters and meetings. Each contains: the thesis, the outcome,
and the transferable lesson. Study these to calibrate your judgment.

## Successful Investments

### See's Candy (1972) — The Franchise Epiphany
- **Cost:** $25M for a business with $7M tangible assets, $4.2M pre-tax earnings
- **Thesis:** Untapped pricing power, beloved brand, exceptional manager (Chuck Huggins),
  capital-light model earning 25%+ on tangible equity
- **Outcome:** By 1991: sales $196M, pre-tax earnings $42.4M, tangible net worth only $25M.
  Total pre-tax profits distributed over 20 years: $410M. Required only $18M incremental capital.
- **Lesson:** A business earning 25%+ on tangible assets has enormous economic goodwill. That
  goodwill grows nominally with inflation (unlike tangible assets which must be replaced at
  higher cost). This single investment taught Buffett to pay up for quality. "We made
  significant money in certain common stocks because of the lessons we learned at See's."

### GEICO (1976-1995) — Crisis + Franchise = Opportunity
- **Cost:** $45.7M total for ~33% stake (1976-1980), then $2.3B for remaining 50% (1995)
- **Thesis:** Near-bankruptcy from underpricing errors, but the fundamental business advantage
  (direct distribution = 15-30% structural cost advantage) remained intact. Distinguished from
  a "turnaround" — the franchise was sound; only the financial crisis was temporary.
- **Outcome:** Original $45.7M became worth over $1B before the buyout. The two-variable
  compensation formula (policy growth + profitability) aligned thousands of employees.
- **Lesson:** The combination of exceptional franchise + temporary crisis + bargain price is
  the ideal setup. "An extraordinary business franchise with a localized excisable cancer should
  be distinguished from the true turnaround situation."

### Coca-Cola (1988) — Inevitable Business at Fair Price
- **Cost:** ~$1.3B for ~7% stake
- **Thesis:** "The most valuable franchise in the world." Untapped pricing power, global growth
  runway, capital-light model, share buybacks increasing Berkshire's ownership percentage.
  The brand is a lollapalooza of operant conditioning, Pavlovian association, social proof,
  and universal availability.
- **Outcome:** By 1991, Coke + Gillette alone accounted for $1.6B of Berkshire's $2.1B net
  worth increase. Held for 35+ years. Share of undistributed earnings grew from $69M (1991)
  to hundreds of millions annually.
- **Lesson:** When the business is truly inevitable, the right price is "fair" — you don't need
  "cheap." Time is the friend of the wonderful business.

### Nebraska Furniture Mart (1983) — Scale + Hustle = Unbeatable
- **Cost:** $55M for 90% ownership. No audit, no inventory check.
- **Thesis:** Mrs. B built the business from $500 to $100M+ sales. NFM's competitive advantage:
  buy brilliantly + operate at expense ratios competitors can't dream of + pass savings to
  customers. Gross margin half of Levitz; operating costs 16.5% vs. 35.6%.
- **Moat test:** "I'd rather wrestle grizzlies than compete with Mrs. B and her progeny."
  Dillard's, upon entering Omaha: "We don't want to compete with them."
- **Outcome:** Sales grew from $100M to $257M+ by 1995. Competitors avoided Omaha entirely.
- **Lesson:** The ideal business has exceptional value to customers (lowest prices + best
  selection) which translates into exceptional economics for owners. Scale advantages in
  retail compound: buying power, talent investment, and transaction data all reinforce.

### Capital Cities/ABC (1985) — The Managerial Twofer
- **Cost:** $517.5M for 3M shares (17% stake) to help fund Cap Cities' acquisition of ABC
- **Thesis:** Tom Murphy was a "twofer" — exceptional at both operations and capital allocation.
  Buffett would do better as a passive 17% owner under Murphy than as 100% owner with lesser
  management.
- **Outcome:** Doubled in ~2 years to $1.035B. When partially sold in 1993 at $630/share
  (vs. $172.50 cost), gain was 265%.
- **Lesson:** Exceptional managers who are also exceptional capital allocators are the rarest
  and most valuable combination. Finding one justifies paying a fair price.

### Scott & Fetzer (1986) — Cash Machine
- **Cost:** $315.2M (book value $172.6M, premium $142.6M)
- **Outcome:** Over nine years, paid Berkshire dividends totaling $1.03B while book value
  remained at $94M. Return on equity ranked #1 among Fortune 500.
- **Lesson:** The premium "overpayment" was amortized annually through goodwill charges,
  making Berkshire's reported earnings look worse — while intrinsic value soared. GAAP
  distorts the economics of buying good businesses.

### Apple (2016+) — Consumer Habit as Moat
- **Thesis:** Munger's observation (2019 meeting): "In my family, the people who have Apple
  phones, it's the last thing they'll give up." The investment thesis is switching cost through
  deep daily habit, not technology leadership.
- **On missing Google:** "We could see in our own operations how well Google advertising was
  working. And we just sat there sucking our thumbs. Maybe Apple was atonement." (2019)
- **Buyback amplifier:** Each dollar Apple spends buying back stock at lower prices increases
  Berkshire's ownership percentage without cost. Same mechanism as Coke in the 1990s.

### Occidental Petroleum (2022) — Reading a Presentation
- **How it happened:** "I read Vicki Hollub's analyst presentation over a weekend. What she was
  saying made nothing but sense. I decided within two weeks." Berkshire bought 14% of the company.
- **Market structure insight:** Four index funds owned ~40% and weren't selling. Berkshire was
  buying 14% of the 60% that was available — "traded by people gambling." Short-term gambling
  enabled long-term acquisition.

### Japan Trading Companies (2019+) — Value Hiding in Plain Sight
- **Thesis:** "We looked at their financial records and were amazed at the low prices."
  Extremely cheap valuations, high dividends, owner-oriented management (far less aggressive
  compensation than U.S. counterparts), yen-denominated debt as built-in currency hedge.
- **Cost:** $13.8B aggregate. **Market value at 2024 yearend:** $23.5B.
- **Lesson:** Sometimes the best opportunities are in familiar, well-run businesses trading at
  low valuations in overlooked markets.

## Mistakes and Lessons

### Berkshire Textiles (1965-1985) — 21 Years of Bad Economics
- **Mistake:** Buying a commodity manufacturer because the price was cheap. Then staying 21 years
  because sunk cost psychology and loyalty to employees made it hard to leave.
- **Economics:** Undifferentiated product, chronic overcapacity, no pricing power, high capital
  intensity, relentless foreign competition. Even excellent managers couldn't overcome it.
- **Lesson:** "When a management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a
  reputation for poor fundamental economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains
  intact." (1980) The "easy" business at a "fancy" price is the better route.

### Dexter Shoe (1993) — The Worst Acquisition
- **Cost:** ~$400M+ in Berkshire stock (25,203 shares at ~$16,000+/share)
- **What happened:** Competitive advantage evaporated as shoe manufacturing shifted offshore.
  The business went to essentially zero. The Berkshire stock paid for it compounded beautifully
  for the sellers.
- **Lesson:** Paying stock for a business that fails is doubly punishing — you give away
  appreciating currency for a depreciating asset. Never issue stock unless you receive at
  least equivalent intrinsic value.

### Gen Re — Cultural Drift (2002)
- **What happened:** An enormously successful operation that drifted from underwriting discipline
  because competitors were drifting too. The derivative book was marked-to-model, not marked-to-
  market. "You give traders the ability to create income by putting numbers on paper nobody can
  check, and it can get out of control."
- **Lesson:** Cultural drift is real and deadly. Watch for companies with historically strong
  margins that begin "winning" business by softening standards. Competitive pressure makes
  drift feel rational in the moment.

### Airlines (2016-2020) — Probability Estimate Error
- **Mistake:** Buffett bought airlines believing the industry had consolidated enough to generate
  acceptable returns. COVID destroyed that thesis.
- **Buffett's candor:** "I just decided I'd made a mistake in evaluating — it was an
  understandable mistake. It was a probability-weighted decision. I don't blame the airlines'
  management; I blame my own probability estimate." (2020 meeting)
- **Lesson:** Even with structural improvement, commodity-like businesses with massive fixed costs
  are vulnerable to tail events. The margin of safety on a commodity business must be very large.

### Long-Term Bonds (1979) — Recognizing but Not Acting
- **Mistake:** Buying 15-year bonds while Buffett could intellectually see inflation destroying
  fixed-income returns.
- **Lesson:** "You do not adequately protect yourself by being half awake while others are
  sleeping." Recognizing a problem intellectually but not acting is its own form of mistake.

### Selling GEICO in 1952 — The Inadvisability of Selling Winners
- **Mistake:** Bought GEICO in 1951 at $10,282. Sold in 1952 for $15,259 — a small profit —
  to switch into Western Insurance Securities (trading at 1x earnings).
- **What he missed:** GEICO grew to over $1B in value. Eventually bought back at $45.7M and
  then $2.3B.
- **Lesson:** "This act of infidelity taught me about the inadvisability of selling a stake in
  an identifiably-wonderful company." (1995)

### Anchoring on Price (Various)
- **From 2004 meeting:** "There have been times when I started buying at X and it went to X
  plus an eighth — and I quit or waited for it to come back. We've missed billions of dollars
  of profit because I'd gotten anchored to some initial price."
- **Lesson:** If the business is worth $100, paying $71 instead of $70 is irrelevant over a
  20-year holding period. Don't let anchoring on a purchase price override your valuation.

## Pattern Recognition Across Case Studies

**What the winners share:**
1. Durable competitive advantage (moat) that existed before purchase
2. Management that thinks like owners
3. Capital-light economics (high returns on tangible assets)
4. Purchased at a fair or better price
5. Held for a very long time

**What the mistakes share:**
1. Commodity economics that couldn't be overcome by management quality
2. Competitive advantages that proved less durable than assumed
3. Stock used as currency (Dexter) or position held too long (textiles)
4. Failure to act on what was already known (long-term bonds)
5. Cultural drift from discipline (Gen Re)
