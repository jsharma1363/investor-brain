# Investment Checklists

Practical checklists derived from Buffett and Munger's decision-making process.
Use sequentially — if any gate question fails, stop and move on.

## Gate Questions (30 seconds — decide whether to spend time)

- [ ] Can I explain how this business makes money to a 10-year-old?
- [ ] Is this within my circle of competence?
- [ ] Can I reasonably estimate earnings in 5-10 years?
- [ ] Has it been around long enough for a meaningful track record?
- [ ] Does it pass the $10B test? ("Would I want to compete with it if I had
      $10 billion and the best management team?")

If any answer is "no" → too hard pile. No further analysis.

## Business Quality Assessment

### Economic Moat
- [ ] Can this company raise prices without losing customers? (Pricing power test)
- [ ] What is the source? (Brand / switching costs / network effects / cost advantage / regulatory)
- [ ] Is the moat widening, stable, or narrowing over time?
- [ ] How long has this competitive advantage existed?
- [ ] Is it dependent on a single person, patent, or temporary condition?
- [ ] What well-funded competitor tried and failed to replicate it?
- [ ] Could the internet or technology destroy it? (Newspapers lost their moat this way)

### Unit Economics
- [ ] Returns on invested capital (ROIC) over last 10 years — consistently above 15%?
- [ ] Returns on incremental capital above cost of capital?
- [ ] Capital-light or capital-heavy? (See's needed $8M for $196M sales; textiles needed $17M+
      for mediocre results)
- [ ] Gross margins stable or improving?
- [ ] Consistent free cash flow generation?
- [ ] What do reinvested dollars earn? (Scott & Fetzer returned $1.03B in dividends on a $315M
      purchase over nine years)

### Industry Structure
- [ ] Growing, stable, or declining?
- [ ] Consolidating or fragmenting?
- [ ] Commodity or differentiated?
- [ ] Winner-take-all or winner-take-most dynamics?
- [ ] What is the threat of disruption?
- [ ] Does the industry have chronic overcapacity? (If so, returns will be poor regardless
      of management quality — the textile lesson)

## Management Evaluation

### Integrity
- [ ] Do they communicate honestly about both successes AND failures?
- [ ] Significant insider ownership — increasing or decreasing?
- [ ] Under-promise and over-deliver?
- [ ] Related-party transactions reasonable?
- [ ] Do their actions match their words over the last 5+ years?
- [ ] Would they pass the Newspaper Test? (Comfortable seeing their actions on the front page?)

### Capital Allocation Track Record
- [ ] Have past acquisitions created value? (Most destroy it — "Don't ask the barber if you
      need a haircut")
- [ ] Are buybacks done below intrinsic value or at any price?
- [ ] Is dividend policy rational given reinvestment opportunities?
- [ ] Do they resist the institutional imperative? (Empire building, unnecessary diversification,
      following peers)
- [ ] Do they hold appropriate cash reserves?
- [ ] Does every retained dollar create more than a dollar of market value over time?

### Compensation
- [ ] Is executive pay aligned with long-term per-share outcomes (not total revenue)?
- [ ] Are stock options reasonable? Are they expensed properly?
- [ ] Would you want this person managing YOUR money?
- [ ] Compare compensation to the Japanese trading companies standard: "far less aggressive
      than their U.S. counterparts"

### Culture
- [ ] Does bad news travel fast? (Persian Messenger Syndrome kills companies)
- [ ] Is there evidence of "cultural drift" from discipline? (Gen Re warning sign)
- [ ] How do they treat acquired managers? (Berkshire: "We find .400 hitters and don't tell
      them how to swing")

## Valuation Checklist

### Owner Earnings Calculation
- [ ] Start with reported net income
- [ ] Add back: depreciation and amortization
- [ ] Subtract: average annual maintenance capex (not growth capex)
- [ ] Subtract: changes in working capital requirements
- [ ] Subtract: stock-based compensation (it IS a real cost — the Quant Tech lesson)
- [ ] Result = owner earnings

### Intrinsic Value Estimation
- [ ] What are current normalized owner earnings?
- [ ] What is a conservative growth rate for the next 10 years?
- [ ] What discount rate reflects your best alternative? (Opportunity cost, not WACC)
- [ ] What is the terminal value assumption? (Is it reasonable?)
- [ ] Intrinsic value per share?
- [ ] Current price vs. intrinsic value — margin of safety at least 25-30%?
- [ ] No P/E cutoff — "We have no cutoff whatsoever" (2000 meeting)

### Sanity Checks
- [ ] Would a sensible businessperson pay this price for the entire company?
- [ ] If the stock market closed for 10 years, would I still be comfortable owning this?
  (Island Test)
- [ ] Does the implied growth rate make sense given industry dynamics?
- [ ] Am I paying for growth that will actually create free cash, or growth that will
      consume all incremental capital?

## Risk / Inversion Checklist

"Invert, always invert." — Jacobi via Munger

### The Kill Shot Test
- [ ] What single event could permanently destroy this business?
- [ ] How likely is that event?
- [ ] Could this business survive a severe 2-3 year recession?
- [ ] Is there customer concentration risk? (>20% revenue from one source)
- [ ] Is there technological obsolescence risk?
- [ ] Is there regulatory risk that could impair the business model?
- [ ] Is there key-person risk?
- [ ] What is the balance sheet risk? (Debt levels, maturity schedule, covenants)

### Red Flags (Any One May Be a Deal-Breaker)
- [ ] Growing earnings with declining cash flow
- [ ] Frequent changes in accounting methods
- [ ] Revenue recognition that front-loads income
- [ ] Accounts receivable growing faster than revenue
- [ ] Frequent "one-time" charges that recur every year
- [ ] Excessive non-GAAP metrics that always look better than GAAP
- [ ] Management that won't discuss risks or failures
- [ ] Acquisitions funded primarily by stock issuance (Dexter Shoe warning)
- [ ] EBITDA emphasis ("Depreciation is a real expense — the machinery really does wear out")
- [ ] Derivative book that is "marked-to-model" not "marked-to-market" (Gen Re warning)

## Psychological Audit (Before Committing Capital)

### Am I Being Rational?
- [ ] Why is this available at this price? What does the market know?
- [ ] Am I suffering from social proof? ("Everyone else is buying it")
- [ ] Am I suffering from envy? ("Others are making money and I'm not")
- [ ] Am I anchored to a past price or a prior thesis?
- [ ] Have I genuinely engaged with the best bear case?
- [ ] Am I in love with the company/CEO? (Liking tendency)
- [ ] Have I talked publicly about this — creating commitment bias?
- [ ] Is recency bias (recent performance, recent news) driving my analysis?
- [ ] Would I buy this today at this price if I didn't already own it?

### Lollapalooza Check
- [ ] Are multiple biases working in the same direction? (If so, extreme caution or
      extreme opportunity — figure out which)
- [ ] Are multiple fundamental factors aligned? (If so, this may warrant a larger position)

## Position Sizing

- [ ] Is this my highest-conviction idea? (If yes, size accordingly — Buffett concentrates.
      "If you find three wonderful businesses, you're securely rich.")
- [ ] Could I handle a 50% drawdown without selling?
- [ ] Does position size reflect both confidence AND business quality?
- [ ] 20-punch-card test: would I use one of my 20 lifetime punches on this?

## Decision Record (Write This Down BEFORE Buying)

1. **Thesis:** Why am I buying? (2-3 sentences max)
2. **Key assumptions:** What must be true for this to work?
3. **Kill criteria:** What would make me sell? (Define BEFORE buying — pre-commitment
   defeats inconsistency-avoidance bias)
4. **Intrinsic value range:** Low / base / high case
5. **Price paid and margin of safety**
6. **Review date:** When will I re-evaluate the thesis?
7. **Inversion:** The single biggest risk and how likely it is
