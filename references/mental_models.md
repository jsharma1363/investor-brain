# Mental Models

Charlie Munger's latticework of mental models and Buffett's key frameworks.
The core insight: you must have models from multiple disciplines and use them in combination.
"To the man with only a hammer, every problem looks like a nail."

## The Latticework: Models by Discipline

### Mathematics
- **Compound interest** — The most powerful force in finance. A business that reinvests at high
  rates over long periods is worth far more than one that cannot. Tax-efficient holding allows
  compounding to work uninterrupted.
- **Probability and expected value** — Every decision is a probability-weighted outcome. The
  stock market is a pari-mutuel system — the question is never "is this good?" but "is the
  price wrong given the true probability distribution?" You don't need to be right often —
  you need the bet size to match the edge.
- **Base rates** — Before analyzing specifics, ask: what is the historical success rate of
  similar situations? Most acquisitions destroy value. Most turnarounds fail. Most startups
  die. Start with the base rate, then adjust.
- **Permutations/combinations** — Multiple independent small risks combine to create larger
  aggregate risk. Aviation safety improved by recognizing compound failure modes.

### Psychology (see cognitive_biases.md for the full 25)
- **Two-track analysis** — Every decision should be analyzed on two tracks simultaneously:
  (1) rational/logical: what do the facts say? (2) psychological: what biases might be
  distorting my analysis or the market's pricing? Most investors use only Track 1.
- **The Lollapalooza Effect** — When multiple psychological tendencies combine in the same
  direction, they don't add — they multiply. Bubbles, panics, and Milgram-experiment-level
  irrationality come from lollapalooza combinations, not single biases.
- **Pavlovian conditioning** — Brands are conditioning machines. Coca-Cola doesn't just sell a
  drink — it associates the drink with happiness, refreshment, achievement, and patriotism
  over decades. These associations are durable because they've been systematically reinforced.

### Microeconomics
- **Advantages of scale** — Four distinct mechanisms: (1) fixed costs spread over more units,
  (2) information/data advantages from volume, (3) social proof/bandwagon effects favoring the
  leader, (4) advertising efficiency at scale. All are self-reinforcing.
- **Disadvantages of scale** — Bureaucracy, rigidity, inability to adapt. Antidote: extreme
  decentralization (the Berkshire model — subsidiaries run themselves with minimal HQ).
- **Competitive destruction** — Most businesses eventually face destruction from a better product
  or lower-cost competitor. The best businesses "surf" from wave to wave. Key question: what
  would kill this business? How long before it happens?
- **Winner-take-all dynamics** — In many markets, the dominant player captures disproportionate
  profit. The second-best newspaper in a city captures almost no advertising. Identify these
  markets and bet on the winner.
- **Tragedy of the commons** — Individual rational actors collectively destroy shared resources.
  Industries with commodity products and multiple competitors tend toward value destruction
  through price competition. Avoid these except during extreme distress.

### Biology / Evolution
- **Natural selection as business analogy** — Businesses exist in competitive ecosystems. Those
  with genuine advantages survive; those without are selected against. Think about what the
  landscape looks like in 10-20 years.
- **Genetic heuristics** — Many psychological biases exist because they served survival in small
  tribes. Loss aversion was adaptive when losing food meant death. In markets, it causes selling
  winners too early and holding losers too long.

### Engineering
- **Margin of safety** — Engineers build bridges that hold 3x the expected load. Apply the same
  to investments. If intrinsic value is $100, don't pay more than $65-70.
- **Checklists** — Aviation accident rates dropped dramatically from systematic checklists, not
  better pilots. Use a mental checklist for every investment. (See checklists.md.)
- **Redundancy** — Critical systems have backup systems. Great businesses have multiple moat
  sources, not just one. A single-moat business is fragile.

### Physics
- **Critical mass / tipping points** — Systems behave linearly until a threshold, then change
  character completely. A brand or network approaching critical mass is extremely valuable.
  One just below it may be worthless.
- **Feedback loops** — Positive: success breeds success (network effects, brand momentum).
  Negative: failure breeds failure (bank runs, margin calls). Recognize which loop is operating.

### History
- **Pattern recognition from cycles** — "There are certain things that frequently come to a bad
  end, but frequently look extremely good in the short run." (2016 meeting) Seeing multiple
  cycles is the only defense against chain-letter structures in new disguises.
- **Darwin's method** — He immediately wrote down any observation contradicting his theory,
  knowing his brain would otherwise discard it. This systematic counter-evidence collection is
  the antidote to confirmation bias.

## Buffett's Key Decision Frameworks

### The Three Filters (Sequential)
1. **Can I understand it?** (Circle of competence) — If no, stop. "The two things we're
   responsible for: keep good managers motivated, and allocate capital. Aside from that,
   we play bridge." (1994 meeting)
2. **Does it have a durable competitive advantage?** (Moat) — If no, stop.
3. **Is the management trustworthy and able?** — If no, stop.
4. **Is the price sensible?** (Margin of safety) — If no, wait.

All four must be yes. Any single "no" is a pass.

### Mr. Market (1987)
"Imagine a partner named Mr. Market who offers to buy or sell every day. Sometimes his price
is plausible; often it's silly. He's there to serve you, not to guide you. If his quotation
is uninteresting today, he'll be back tomorrow. It will be disastrous if you fall under his
influence."

### The One-Punch Card
Imagine a card with only 20 punches for your entire investing lifetime. Every investment
decision punches a hole. Would you make the same decisions? This promotes concentration and
patience over diversification and activity.

### The Island Test
"If I were stranded on an island for 10 years with no ability to trade, would I be comfortable
owning this business?" If yes, it might be a good investment.

### The Newspaper Test
"Would I be comfortable seeing this action reported on the front page of the local newspaper,
written by a smart but unfriendly reporter?" If not, don't do it.

### Owner Earnings vs. Reported Earnings
Reported earnings include accounting fictions. Owner earnings = what cash an owner could
extract without impairing the business. Always convert to owner earnings before valuing.
The "look-through" earnings concept: Berkshire's true earning power includes retained earnings
at partially-owned businesses that don't appear in GAAP.

### The $10 Billion Moat Test
"How would I like, assuming I had ample capital and skilled personnel, to compete with this
business?" If the answer is "I'd rather wrestle grizzlies," the moat is wide.

### Economic Goodwill vs. Accounting Goodwill (1983 Essay)
- Accounting goodwill = purchase price minus fair value of identifiable assets. Amortized.
- Economic goodwill = the capitalized value of returns on tangible assets that exceed market
  rates. This is the real thing — and it grows with inflation.
- A low-tangible-asset business (See's: $8M to do $100M+ in sales) only needs slightly more
  capital when prices double. A capital-intensive business needs proportionally more capital.
  Economic goodwill of See's-type businesses grows nominally with inflation while high-capital
  businesses see goodwill destroyed by inflation requirements.

### The Franchise vs. Business vs. Commodity Hierarchy (1991)
- **Franchise:** Needed/desired product, no close substitute, no price regulation = aggressive
  pricing power, high returns. Can tolerate bad management.
- **Business:** Earns good profits only as low-cost operator or when supply is tight. Can be
  killed by poor management.
- **Commodity:** Undifferentiated product, price set by market, returns at or below cost of
  capital. Avoid.

### The Two-Category Acquirer Framework (1981)
- **Category 1:** Companies that only buy businesses well-adapted to inflation — easy price
  increases, low capital requirements. Managers of ordinary ability in Category 1 achieve
  excellent results.
- **Category 2:** "Managerial superstars who can recognize the rare prince disguised as a toad."
  Tom Murphy, Henry Singleton. Extremely rare.

## Munger's Multidisciplinary Approach

The key insight: **no single model is sufficient.** Use models from multiple disciplines:

| Discipline | Key Models | Investing Application |
|---|---|---|
| Psychology | Incentives, social proof, loss aversion, lollapalooza | Management and market behavior |
| Economics | Supply/demand, competitive advantage, opportunity cost | Business analysis and valuation |
| Mathematics | Compounding, probability, base rates | Position sizing and expected value |
| Engineering | Margin of safety, redundancy, checklists, breakpoints | Risk management |
| Biology | Evolution, adaptation, ecosystems | Industry dynamics and disruption |
| Physics | Critical mass, tipping points, feedback loops | Network effects and scaling |
| History | Cycles, analogies, human nature | Pattern recognition |

## The Munger Decision Process (Integrated)

1. **Circle check** — Is this within my competence? If not → too hard pile. Stop.
2. **Business quality** — Tier 1 (inevitable), Tier 2 (good), Tier 3 (commodity)? Most are Tier 3.
3. **Moat analysis** — Source, durability, direction (widening or narrowing)?
4. **Incentive mapping** — How are decision-makers paid? Track record on capital allocation?
5. **Financial reality** — Convert to owner earnings. Assess balance sheet.
6. **Valuation** — What would a rational private buyer pay? Margin of safety?
7. **Psychological audit** — Which biases might distort my analysis or the market's pricing?
8. **Lollapalooza check** — Are multiple factors aligned? How large should the bet be?
9. **Inversion** — What would make this fail? Have I genuinely engaged with failure modes?
10. **Decision** — Yes / No / Too Hard. If Yes, how large? (20-punch-card discipline)
