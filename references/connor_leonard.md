# Connor Leonard / IMC — Moat Taxonomy & Capital Compounding

Distilled from IMC 2017 Annual Review, Saber Capital guest post (2016), and Q&A with
Bluegrass Capital (2016). Connor Leonard runs the public portfolio at Investors Management
Corporation (IMC), a privately-held company investing with internal capital as a long-term
business owner. Track record: 18% CAGR pre-tax, October 2011 to September 2016.

---

## Core Philosophy: The Long Game

IMC invests on a 5-to-10-year time horizon because **the playing field five-plus years out
in public markets is far, far less competitive** — giving IMC a much greater chance of
success. This isn't about being smarter. It's about playing a different game with a higher
probability of success.

### Why the long game works

| | Peers | IMC Portfolio |
|---|---|---|
| **Time Horizon** | <1 Year | 5-10 Year |
| **Key Factor** | Multiple expansion, sentiment change | Business performance |
| **ROIC** | Irrelevant | Crucial |

Short-term returns are driven by **external** factors (multiples, sentiment) that are noisy
and unpredictable. Long-term returns are driven by **internal** factors (business performance,
ROIC) that are more analyzable and durable.

**The edge is structural, not informational.** Most investors claim to be long-term but their
behavior is short-term (performance reviews, redemption cycles, career risk). IMC operates
with permanent capital and no external LPs — eliminating the agency problem entirely.

## Investing Like an Industrialist

IMC operates with the **mentality of a private industrialist investing their own capital.**
No geography, sector, or market cap constraints. Three stocks, three hundred stocks, or
no stocks — whatever the opportunity set demands.

This flexibility, paired with a commitment to constant improvement, should result in an
informed allocation of capital to the best possible opportunities — regardless of whether
the investment fits neatly into a style box.

**Key insight for moat analysis:** Most institutional investors are constrained by mandates
(US large cap, growth, value). These constraints create mispricings because entire categories
of businesses are ignored or misjudged — especially international small/mid caps and
businesses that don't fit standard classification. IMC found its way to a German online pet
retailer (zooplus) through attending the Daily Journal meeting, hearing Munger talk about
China, and doing a deep independent research dive. The process matters as much as the
framework.

## The Four Business Categories (Moat Taxonomy)

This is the core framework. Every business falls into one of four categories based on
**how it deploys capital and whether that deployment widens the moat.**

### 1. Legacy Moat — Returning Capital

Entrenched position with strong, consistent profitability **relative to the prior invested
capital.** However, few opportunities to deploy incremental capital at similarly high rates.
Management returns the majority of earnings to owners via dividends.

- Revenue and earnings are protected and predictable
- But limited growth opportunities — no reinvestment runway
- Over 80% of earnings distributed as dividends
- **Examples:** Coca-Cola, Hershey's

**Investing application:** These are cash cows. Wonderful businesses but limited compounding.
The return is yield-driven (dividend + modest growth), not reinvestment-driven. Fair price
matters enormously because there's no growth engine to bail you out. Maps to Mauboussin's
mature life cycle (positive operating CF, negative investing CF, negative financing CF).

### 2. Legacy Moat — "Outsider" Management

All the characteristics of a Legacy Moat, but management decides to **retain all capital
and deploy it into new businesses through a focused M&A program.** The home office
effectively serves as an internal private equity fund, using permanent capital supplied
by the operating companies to fund a disciplined acquisition effort.

- William Thorndike's "The Outsiders" model
- Exceptional capital allocators pairing stable cash flows with opportunistic M&A
- The result can be remarkable compounding of shareholder capital

**Investing application:** The moat is in the operating businesses but the compounding is in
management's allocation skill. You're underwriting the CEO as capital allocator, not just the
business. If management is world-class, this is a Core Holding. Maps to Mauboussin's capital
allocation framework — which of the eight levers is management pulling, and at what ROIIC?

### 3. Reinvestment Moat

**The rare company that can deploy incremental capital at high rates within the current
business.** Profits are plowed right back into growing the existing business, simplifying
the task of capital allocation.

- Self-reinforcing business models: scale advantages or network effects that widen the
  competitive advantage over time
- **Example:** Walmart in the early 1970s — 51 locations, aggressively growing. Today 11,000
  locations, sales and net income up over 5,000x from 1972 levels.
- An emerging Reinvestment Moat with a proven model and long runway for expansion has the
  potential to be an exceptional Core Holding.

**Investing application:** This is the compounder. The moat widens as the business grows
because growth itself strengthens the competitive position. The key test: does each dollar
reinvested earn above WACC AND make the next dollar of reinvestment more defensible?
This is where Mauboussin's ROIIC is most critical — you need ROIIC > WACC sustainably,
and the reinvestment runway must be long.

**This is the META thesis.** 3.3B DAP is a reinvestment moat: every dollar spent on AI
infrastructure serves more users, generates more data, improves ad targeting, which funds
more infrastructure. The flywheel widens the moat as it spins.

### 4. Capital-Light Compounder

Revenue and earnings grow over a long period while **requiring minimal or zero incremental
capital investment.** The competitive advantage is rooted in intangible assets or network
effects, so future growth is not contingent on large capital expenditures.

- Grow AND distribute massive cash to owners — a dream combination
- **Examples:** See's Candies, Visa, Moody's
- If paired with industry tailwinds and shareholder-friendly management, this is an ideal
  Core Holding

**Investing application:** The holy grail. Growth without capital consumption. These businesses
generate FCF far in excess of what they need to grow. The investor gets both compounding
AND cash return. Maps to Buffett's See's Candy archetype — $8M in tangible assets generating
$13M after-tax.

## The Four Categories Applied to Portfolio Construction

| Category | Return Driver | Capital Need | Investor Gets | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Legacy — Returning | Yield | Low (returns capital) | Dividends + modest growth | Long but slow |
| Legacy — Outsider | M&A allocation | Variable (acquisitions) | Compounding via management skill | Depends on CEO |
| Reinvestment Moat | Internal reinvestment | High (but earns high returns) | Compounding via business flywheel | Longest runway |
| Capital-Light | Intangible/network moat | Minimal | Growth + massive FCF | Longest + most cash |

**The ranking for long-term investors:** Capital-Light Compounder ≥ Reinvestment Moat >
Legacy Outsider > Legacy Returning Capital. The first two compound intrinsically. The third
depends on management genius. The fourth is yield, not compounding.

## Position Sizing & Portfolio Construction (Bluegrass Q&A)

### Concentrated portfolio, disciplined sizing
- 10-15 companies at a time
- Minimum 5% weighting, upside cap of 10% at cost per position
- ~2% starter positions for companies that are "highly desirable on most traits but still
  have some nagging concern around valuation, industry structure, or capital allocation"
- Goal: continue learning about the company while not missing any upside move
- Capital at risk makes you a more efficient and focused analyst

### Cash as a free call option
Cash is a standalone position in every portfolio with no expiration date — not a byproduct
of other holdings. This connects to Mauboussin's Kelly Criterion: when no bet meets the
threshold, the optimal bet size is zero. Cash is the default, not an afterthought.

### The Wall of Shame
A grid where the horizontal axis is reasons for losses and the vertical axis is company
names. "There is unfortunately a lot of overlap on some of the squares." This grid forces
a written pre-mortem for each new investment decision — which Leonard considers as important
as, or more important than, the primary investment thesis.

**Wall of Shame red flags include:** business declining or not growing; can't clearly
articulate revenue model; competitive environment in flux or not stable; complexity
increasing, not decreasing; conflicts between various key stakeholders; confusing related
party structures; a startup funded with $1B could meaningfully disrupt the company; customer
concentration; don't know where product demand will be in five years; entered the investment
for a short-term trade; heavily dependent on commodity prices or interest rates; inexperienced
and non-proven management team; no known catalysts; no notable investors have ownership; not
materially free cash flow positive; not returning capital to shareholders; position size too
large; upside structurally capped.

## Qualitative Screening (No Screeners)

Leonard does not use quantitative screening tools. If he did: >20% ROIC, gross margin
consistency or expansion, and share count consistency or reduction.

Instead, he uses overlapping qualitative traits:
- Management teams highly regarded in their industry and long-tenured
- Plain-spoken annual shareholder letters (not detailed but honest)
- Management consistently discusses capital allocation options and priorities
- Openly discusses past investment failures and successes
- Transparent shareholder relations (conference transcripts on front page of IR site)

### The Peter Lynch Brand Test
"Any brand someone chooses to display proudly on their chest is probably owned by a good
company. One sees Mickey Mouse and Coca Cola paraphernalia all the time, but no one wears
a Comcast T-shirt (although I see lots of Google Fiber t-shirts)." This is Cialdini's social
proof applied to brand moat identification — if customers voluntarily advertise for you,
that's share of mind made visible.

## What Makes a High Quality Company

### Tom Gayner's criteria (endorsed by Leonard)
A good investment must have:
1. Company generates free cash flow (or clear path to doing so)
2. High returns on invested capital
3. Talented existing management team
4. Can be acquired at a reasonable price
5. Has numerous attractive internal reinvestment opportunities

**The last criterion is the most important** because it proves the existence of the first
three. This is Leonard's Reinvestment Moat in Gayner's language.

### "High quality" correlates with high ROIC
For high ROIC to be present, the company must have some combination of defensive moat
qualities. A list of moat sources with examples:
- Low cost provider (Costco)
- Industry currency / network (Moody's, Facebook)
- Brand loyalty (Hershey)
- Switching costs (Fiserv)
- Patents / IP (Disney)
- Regulatory license (Rolls Royce)
- Culture (Fastenal)

### The correct equity market multiple
When the top quartile of public companies earn ROIC of 20%+ and the risk-free rate is ~2%,
the correct average equity market multiple is 15x to 20x earnings. Companies employing very
little tangible capital (like Facebook) may actually justify 30-40x. Market participants are
not recognizing this shift — resulting in high-quality companies being undervalued and
average companies being overvalued. This is the expectations approach (Mauboussin) applied
to market-wide mispricing.

## Research Process

1. Read multiple years of annual reports, proxies, conference transcripts for the target
   company and its closest competitors
2. Read industry news flow from the prior few years via trade journals
3. Discuss the business and management team with other analysts and employees of competitor
   firms
4. Start with "I hate this company" attitude — try to prove that thought correct
5. If after a week of reading you can't poke a lot of holes in the long thesis, you know
   it may be worthwhile. Take a starter position.
6. During the holding period, look for increased confidence or a series of new questions
   that eventually change your mind

### The anti-research process
"I spend most of my time reading shareholder letters, annual reports, transcripts, and trade
journals, and probably not enough time reading books." This is primary source research, not
secondary analysis. Reading what management actually says, not what analysts say about them.

## Mental Management

- Observe price movements as little as possible during market hours
- Favor securities with high levels of daily liquidity

  It's a coin flip whether you embarrass yourself in public (lose money)."

## Connecting to Mauboussin and Porter

Leonard's taxonomy fills a gap between Porter (industry structure) and Mauboussin (firm
economics). Porter tells you if the industry is attractive. Mauboussin tells you if ROIC
is above WACC. **Leonard tells you what happens with the cash** — which determines whether
high ROIC translates into compounding or just dividends.

| Framework | Question Answered |
|---|---|
| Porter Five Forces | Is this a good industry? |
| Mauboussin ROIC/Moat | Does this company earn excess returns? |
| Leonard Categories | What does management do with those excess returns? |

The complete analytical chain: Porter → Mauboussin → Leonard → Valuation (expectations approach).

## Other Key Insights

### Market Hedges as Long-Term Discipline
IMC maintained market hedges throughout 2017 despite being long-biased. Not a market call —
a "long-biased mindset" to reduce overall exposure during frothy periods without selling
core holdings. This preserves the tax efficiency of long-duration compounding while managing
drawdown risk.

### The Industrialist Research Process
- Read 100 annual reports and 25 books per year
- Attend conferences (Daily Journal meeting led to the China thesis which led to zooplus)
- Meet with at least 10 business professionals per year
- Conversations with knowledgeable operators yield durable insights the market overlooks

### The zooplus Case Study (Reinvestment Moat in action)
- European online pet products. 80% recurring, habitual purchases. Low variance.
- "Scale economies shared" model (Costco playbook) — pass scale efficiencies to customers
  as lower prices, which drives more volume, which creates more scale
- Market share exceeding 40% in each year since going public in 2008
- Growing 18-29% YoY in Amazon's strongest European markets
- 36% ROCE because the efficient model turns over capital >20x per year
- Management consciously sacrificing current profitability to widen the long-term moat
- Chewy.com acquisition by PetSmart for $3.35B (largest e-commerce acquisition in history
  at the time) validated the business model at 0.75x 2018 forward sales — zooplus traded
  at a discount to this comparable

**Why this matters:** The zooplus thesis demonstrates every concept in the skill simultaneously:
reinvestment moat (Leonard), cost leadership driving demand-side scale (Porter), ROIC well
above WACC with long runway (Mauboussin), and the "scale economies shared" approach as a
Lollapalooza moat-widening mechanism (Cialdini — reciprocation with customers who get value,
building loyalty that compounds).
