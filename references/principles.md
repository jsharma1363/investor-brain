# Core Investing Principles

Distilled from Buffett's shareholder letters (1965-2024), Berkshire annual meetings, and
Munger's talks. Grounded in specific examples from the source material.

## On Business Quality

- **Owner earnings are what matter.** Net income is an accounting fiction. Owner earnings =
  net income + depreciation/amortization - maintenance capex - changes in working capital -
  stock-based compensation (it's a real cost). This is your starting point for valuation.
- **Pricing power is the single most important test.** "If you've got the power to raise prices
  without losing business to a competitor, you've got a very good business." (1981) See's Candy
  raised prices every year for decades without losing customers. A box of See's isn't a commodity
  candy — it's an expression of love on Valentine's Day.
- **Seek businesses with durable competitive advantages (moats).** The moat test: "If I had
  $10 billion and the best management team in the world, could I dent this company's competitive
  position?" If the answer is no, the moat is wide.
- **Capital-light businesses are superior.** See's Candy needed only $8M in tangible assets and
  generated $13M after-tax profit by 1983 — earning 160%+ on tangible equity. Capital-intensive
  businesses must plow all earnings back just to maintain unit volume.
- **Simple businesses are preferable.** "There are two kinds of businesses: the first earns 12%
  and you can take it out at the end of the year. The second earns 12% but all the excess cash
  must be reinvested. The first is See's Candy; the second is Berkshire's textile operation."
- **An economic franchise has three characteristics** (1991): (1) the product is needed or
  desired, (2) customers perceive no close substitute, (3) it is not subject to price regulation.
  All three = aggressive pricing power and high returns on capital.
- **Turnarounds seldom turn.** (1979) "The same energies and talent are much better employed in
  a good business purchased at a fair price than in a poor business purchased at a bargain price."
  Berkshire Textiles ran 21 years with excellent managers but terrible economics. The reputation
  of the business always remained intact.
- **Stability beats excitement.** "The best business returns are usually achieved by companies
  doing something quite similar today to what they were doing five or ten years ago." (1987)
  A Fortune study found only 25 of 1,000 companies averaged over 20% ROE for a decade — most
  were in mundane businesses.

## On Competitive Advantage (Moats)

Five types of durable moat, with specific examples:

1. **Brand power / consumer mind share** — See's Candies, Coca-Cola, Apple. The customer *wants*
   this specific product and won't substitute. Coca-Cola's moat is designed as a "lollapalooza"
   combining operant conditioning, Pavlovian association, social proof, and ubiquitous availability.
2. **Switching costs** — Apple (daily habit: "it's the last thing they'll give up" — Munger, 2019),
   enterprise software, banking relationships. Once embedded, the pain of switching exceeds any
   benefit.
3. **Network effects** — American Express, Visa, platforms. Value grows with each user.
4. **Cost advantages** — GEICO's direct model saves 15-30% vs. agent-based competitors.
   Nebraska Furniture Mart operates at 16.5% expense ratio vs. 35.6% for Levitz. These structural
   advantages compound over time.
5. **Regulatory / infrastructure barriers** — BNSF Railroad moves 15% of all U.S. ton-miles.
   You cannot build competing parallel track. Utilities, certain licenses.

**Moats can disappear.** Newspapers once had all three franchise characteristics. The internet
destroyed their information monopoly completely — not partially. A moat dependent on being
the *only* conduit for information is destroyed entirely when alternatives emerge.

**Moats can widen or narrow by management action.** Widening (adding products, geographies,
brand extensions done right) is rare and valuable. Most management inadvertently narrows moats
through over-diversification, brand dilution, or poor capital allocation.

## On Valuation

- **Intrinsic value = the discounted value of cash that can be taken out of a business during
  its remaining life.** (1994) "Anyone calculating intrinsic value necessarily comes up with a
  highly subjective figure."
- **Intrinsic value is a range, not a number.** (2007 meeting) "If Charlie and I were each to
  write down what we think intrinsic value of Berkshire is, our figures would not be the same.
  They'd be reasonably close."
- **Margin of safety is non-negotiable.** Build bridges that hold 30,000 pounds to carry 10,000.
  Your analysis will have errors — the margin absorbs them. Required margin is inversely
  proportional to business quality: a highly predictable See's needs less margin than a cyclical
  commodity business.
- **Growth is a component of value, not separate from it.** Growth only creates value when
  return on incremental capital exceeds cost of capital. A company growing at 15% but requiring
  all earnings to fund that growth creates no free cash.
- **The Frozen Corporation test** (Ben Graham, cited 1998 meeting): A company whose charter
  prohibited it from ever paying shareholders or being sold — what is it worth? Zero. A financial
  asset must eventually disgorge cash to have value.
- **No P/E cutoff exists.** (2000 meeting) "We have no cutoff whatsoever. We don't think in
  terms of absolutes that way." GEICO was bought while losing money — the future was
  significantly different from the current situation.
- **The one-foot bar principle.** (1998 meeting) "In Olympic diving, they have a degree of
  difficulty factor. That's not true in investments. You get paid just as well for the most
  simple dive. So we look for one-foot bars to step over."

## On Capital Allocation

- **Every dollar retained must create more than a dollar of market value.** (1983) If management
  can't deploy capital at above-average returns, return it via dividends or buybacks.
- **Share repurchases only make sense below intrinsic value.** GEICO's 1979-80 buyback: reduced
  shares from 34.2M to 21.6M, a 37% reduction, "dramatically enhancing shareholders' interests
  in a business that simply can't be replicated."
- **Never issue stock below intrinsic value.** (1982) "For gold valued as gold cannot be purchased
  intelligently through the utilization of gold valued as lead." Paying stock for Dexter Shoe
  was Buffett's worst acquisition — giving away appreciating currency for a depreciating asset.
- **The institutional imperative is the enemy.** (1989) Organizations resist change, imitate
  peers, pursue activity for its own sake, and CEOs' subordinates will produce justifications
  for whatever the CEO wants to do. Leaders must actively fight this.
- **Float is the engine.** Berkshire's insurance float grew from $17M in 1967 to $171B in 2024.
  At negative or zero cost, this float is equivalent to free equity — invested in businesses
  and stocks, generating the returns that built Berkshire.
- **Doing nothing is a valid allocation.** (1986) "We had no new ideas in the marketable equities
  field... So our main capital allocation moves were to pay off debt and stockpile funds.
  Neither is a fate worse than death."
- **Acquisitions destroy value more often than they create it.** "Don't ask the barber whether
  you need a haircut." (1982) Investment bankers will always find reasons to do deals.

## On Management

- **Integrity, intelligence, energy — in that order.** "If they don't have the first, be sure
  they don't have the other two. If you've got somebody that lacks integrity, you want them to
  be dumb and lazy." (Pete Kiewit's rule, cited 2016 meeting)
- **Look for managers who think like owners.** Do they have significant personal stakes? Do they
  allocate capital rationally? Do they communicate honestly about failures? Buffett's test:
  read the proxy statement — "see how they treat themselves versus shareholders." (1994 meeting)
- **The best managers are candid about failures.** Buffett publicly catalogs his mistakes in
  every annual letter. If annual reports only celebrate successes, be suspicious.
- **"Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome."** (Munger) Before evaluating any
  management decision, map the incentive structure. Options-heavy compensation = incentive to
  boost short-term stock price. Revenue bonuses = acquisitions regardless of price.
- **Persian Messenger Syndrome kills companies.** Organizations that punish bad news create
  information blackouts. Look for cultures where bad news travels fast. Gen Re's "cultural
  drift" — a slow departure from underwriting discipline because competitors were drifting —
  cost Berkshire billions.
- **Bet on the horse, not the jockey** (for great businesses). "In a good business, even a
  mediocre manager can't destroy value quickly. In a commodity business, management quality
  is decisive."

## On Risk and Psychology

- **Risk is the probability of permanent loss of capital, not volatility.** A wonderful business
  bought at a fair price has low risk regardless of its beta.
- **Be fearful when others are greedy, and greedy when others are fearful.** "We have usually
  made our best purchases when apprehensions about some macro event were at a peak." (1994)
- **Inactivity is underrated.** "The big money is not in the buying and the selling, but in the
  waiting." (Munger) Most investors trade too much and think too little.
- **Avoid leverage.** "Any time you combine ignorance and borrowed money, you can get some
  pretty interesting consequences." (1994 meeting) Leverage turns a temporary decline into
  a permanent loss.
- **The "too hard" pile is your best friend.** Three baskets: Yes, No, Too Hard. Most
  investment decisions should be in the Too Hard basket. "We can say 'no' to 90-plus percent
  of all things that come in simply because we have these filters." (1998 meeting)
- **Concentrate, don't diversify** (if you know what you're doing). "If you know how to analyze
  businesses, it's crazy to own 50 stocks. If you find three wonderful businesses in your life,
  you'll get very rich." (1996 meeting) Munger: "Wide diversification is madness."
- **The tax advantage of long-term holding is enormous.** $1 doubling 20 times with annual
  trading at 34% tax = ~$25,250 after-tax. Same $1 doubling 20 times without selling =
  ~$692,000 after one final tax. (1989)

## On Market Behavior

- **Mr. Market is your servant, not your master.** (1987) "He is there to serve you, not to
  guide you. It is his pocketbook, not his wisdom, that you will find useful."
- **Short-term = voting machine. Long-term = weighing machine.** Coke went public at $40 in 1919.
  Fell 50% to $19.50 by end of 1920. By yearend 1993, one share with dividends reinvested
  was worth over $2.1 million.
- **Market downturns are opportunities for the prepared.** "If you're a net buyer of stocks
  over time, you should welcome falling prices the way a hamburger lover welcomes falling
  beef prices."
- **The passive investor's arithmetic is irrefutable.** (2016 meeting) Divide all investors into
  passive and active halves. Passive owns exactly half of everything, earns exactly average,
  with near-zero cost. Active also earns average *gross* — but minus fees. Active, as a group,
  *must* do worse. "It's that simple."

## On Learning and Temperament

- **Read everything.** "In my whole life, I have known no wise people who didn't read all the
  time. None. Zero." (Munger)
- **Think independently.** "You are neither right nor wrong because the crowd agrees or
  disagrees. You're right because your data and reasoning are right." (Graham/Buffett)
- **Temperament is more important than IQ.** The ability to remain rational when others are
  emotional is the single greatest edge in investing.
- **The best investment is in yourself.** (2022 meeting) "If you're the best doctor in town,
  no matter whether people are paying you with a zillion dollars or wheat — they'll give you
  some of what they produce. Your abilities can't be taken away or inflated away."
- **Acknowledge mistakes quickly.** "At least we rub our own noses in our mistakes in blowing
  opportunities. Since those don't hit financial reports, most people don't bother thinking
  about them." (Munger, 2004 meeting)
