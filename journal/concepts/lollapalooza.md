# Lollapalooza Effect

**Last updated:** 2026-04-03

## Definition

When multiple psychological forces or competitive advantages combine, they don't just add — they multiply. Coined by Munger. Formalized in Cialdini's influence framework.

## In Business/Moats

A company with one moat source is defensible. A company where multiple moat sources reinforce each other is a Lollapalooza — the combined effect is far greater than the sum.

## Example: META

META's moat isn't just network effects. It's:
- **Social proof** x **commitment/consistency** x **attention scarcity** x **network effects**

Each force reinforces the others. Users stay because everyone else is there (social proof), because they've invested years of content and connections (commitment), because the platform captures finite attention (scarcity), and because leaving means losing access to the network (switching costs).

Advertisers face their own Lollapalooza: pixel data + custom audiences + institutional knowledge + campaign history.

See [[META]] for full thesis.

## In Investing Psychology

The same Lollapalooza works against investors:
- You bought a stock someone you admire recommended (authority + liking)
- You told your friends about it (commitment/consistency)
- It went up and everyone else bought it too (social proof)
- Now you can't sell even though forward returns are poor

Multiple biases compounding = very hard to override with rational analysis alone.
