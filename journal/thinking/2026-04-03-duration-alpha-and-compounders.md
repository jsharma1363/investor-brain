# Duration, Alpha, and Compounders

**Date:** 2026-04-03

## The Discussion

Started from Paul Enright's duration taxonomy (5 categories from months to decades) and his three-path alpha framework:

1. **Nose for where/when to dig** — knowing which situations warrant deep work before others recognize it
2. **Concentration** — only the very best ideas
3. **No nostalgia** — removing attachment to past performance, focusing only on future expected returns, accepting when market pulls forward returns

## Where I Landed

Enright's framework is largely right but the three paths are **sequential, not parallel**:
- Path 1 feeds path 2 (you can only concentrate correctly if you've identified the right ideas)
- Path 3 is the behavioral discipline that lets paths 1 and 2 work over time

### The Key Tension (Resolved)

Enright's path 3 ("duration isn't the path to anything other than index level returns") seems to conflict with holding a compounder at a good entry price. But the tension resolves cleanly:

**Hold a compounder not because of your entry price, but because from today's price, the forward return still beats alternatives.**

- Entry price is irrelevant to the hold/sell decision
- The compounding IS the alpha — you don't need to trade around it
- But if the market re-rates the compounder (P/E goes from 15x to 30x), path 3 says sell even though the business is still excellent
- The question is always: "What are the forward returns from here?"

### Duration as Alpha Source

Duration IS valid — but only as a byproduct of paths 2 and 3 working together. Hold the concentrated position AND maintain discipline to reassess forward returns without anchoring. Then duration compounds your edge. Duration alone (just not selling) is not a strategy.

## Key Insight

Don't conflate "the compounding is the alpha" with "never sell." The compounding is the alpha **as long as forward returns from current price justify holding.** The moment the market prices in the compounding (pulls forward returns), the math changes.
